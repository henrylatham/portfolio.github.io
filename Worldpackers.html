Worldpackers

UX/UI Designer for SÃ£o Paulo Travel Startup

Problem

Worldpackers seeks to democratise travel by removing the main barrier to entry: money. Volunteers are able to offer their skills & time in exchange for accommodation, meals & other perks.

This means that a Brazilian student who dreams of traveling around Europe & learning English on the way is able to follow their dream, developing themselves - as well as their CV - on the road.

Growing to over 500,000 users in 3 years, since 2016 the company has been focused on growing internationally from its strong base in Latin America, as well as diversifying from its mainly student user base.

The primary issue we faced - and continue to face - is how to retain customers when our users were only able to take a 1+ month trip every few years, or even just once in their lifetime.

Ideation & Role

Most of my time was spent focusing on making incremental changes to the iOS, Android & web platforms.

This involved looking at our quantitative analytics, creating quantitative data through user interviews, usability testing & online feedback forms, creating wireframes & prototypes to test new ideas on users, as well as completing & exporting finald designs & interactions for our developers & other stakeholders.

I was also at the forefront of making our development team more efficient & fully remote, transitioning to a more organised & defined work flow, primarily through daily check-ins through Hangouts & our design stack of Sketch, Slack, InVision, Principle & Zeplin.

Beyond the iterative work, we focused on building a new retention mechanism.

We looked at - and tested - a number of different options, from user stories to regular meet-ups & events for users, whether they were traveling in their home city.

Essentially, without building a community, we would not see virality within the product & needed to constantly acquire new users, rather than exploit our existing user base.

Execution

Our product team worked in 2-week sprints, with myself & our other UX Designer always pushing ourselves to finalise solutions within the first week to then spend the second week wireframing & testing possible product changes for the following sprint.

This ensured a rapid iteration process, as our developers knew precisely what to work on once we arrived at the start of the new sprint, as we had quantitative feedback to inform our decisions about what to work on.

In June, a month of user interviews demonstrated that it made sense to pivot towards offering events & courses in order to create a community with high retention.

However, I disagreed with the direction our product manager was taking & did not think we would execute effectively, so resigned rather than continue on a project I know longer believed in.
