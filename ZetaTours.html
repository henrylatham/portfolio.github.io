Zeta Tours

Website for tour & travel company

Problem

Zeta Tours has, for the last 3 years, suffered from a very high bounce rate. The previous site, although aesthetically pleasing & highly iinteractive, failed to convert prospective clients.

The fundamental flaw was that the target market were in their 50’s-60’s & were not particularly tech-literate.

Therefore the use of icons, filters & an interactive map were all intimidating & unusable for the majority of our visitors.

Furthermore, the sheer number of categories (10-15) and the 100+ itinerary pins on a small map overwhelmed many first-time visitors.

When approached with the website re-build, I therefore focused by attempting to answer the following questions:

1) How do I make the user journey incredibly simple considering the demographic?

2) How do I make the diverse, numerous tours Zeta Tours offer more manageable?

Ideation & Role

I started by delving into the types of websites and technology the target market would be proficient in.

Gmail and Google were almost universally used on a regular basis, so I decided to start there, using Material Design principles as

the foundation of the site.

The minimalist, card-based layout and recognisable iconography would be more easily navigable. It would also instil confidence in our visitors & confidence in our brand.

I tested my ideas by sending mock-ups to those within our target market to see what they would and wouldn’t recognise, iterating accordingly.

I asked questions such as, ‘Would you know what this icon does?’, ‘What would you expect this button to do?’, etc.

Having simplified the overall structure of the user interface, I then focused on simplifying the content.

If we were unable to show all of the content, how best to nudge visitors to our most popular tours?

Using quantitative data from sales and enquiries, I added a ‘Recommendations’ section comprised of our six most popular destinations.

This not only solved the problem of having an empty search page on arrival at the site, but also meant that an empty search (i.e. where no results appeared) would still reveal some options.

By displaying itineraries in a card format, the large amount of photos and text within each was hidden from view, making them less intimidating to our visitors.

Furthermore the simple scroll format required less effort than the previous horizontal slide-show-style format.

Execution

Once designed, I then hired a developer to implement the UI and update the backend (allowing me to add meta-data, making the content recognisable by search engines & reducing the size of photos for faster loading times).

We will be launching the new website at the end of October 2016.
